# ContentCue Now Available in 11 Languages — Read Web Content Without Losing Your Place to Ads

When you read articles on the web, you often lose track of where the actual content ends and where the ads begin. Some ads are obvious. But embedded sponsored content, promoted recommendations, and native advertising are designed to look like part of the article — same font, same column width, same visual style. [ContentCue](/en/contentcue/) solves this by marking ad regions with a coloured border as you read, so you always know which parts of the page are editorial and which are paid placements.

That experience is now available in 11 languages. ContentCue has expanded its full UI localisation to cover English, Spanish, French, Dutch, Portuguese, Russian, Simplified Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Bahasa Indonesia, and Malay — giving web readers worldwide a cleaner, more honest reading experience.

## The Problem ContentCue Addresses

Most ad blockers remove ads entirely. That approach has a side effect: you lose track of the natural reading flow because the page layout shifts, content jumps around, and sometimes the surrounding article context disappears along with the ad it was wrapped around.

ContentCue takes a different approach — it highlights rather than hides. Ads stay in place, but get a coloured border that instantly identifies them as paid content. This does several things:

**You know what you're reading.** When you see a coloured border, you know to treat the content differently — it wasn't written by the publication's editorial team and wasn't selected by an algorithm for your benefit.

**Reading flow stays intact.** The article text doesn't reflow around missing content. You scroll at the same pace, and your reading position stays consistent.

**Resume where you left off.** ContentCue remembers where you paused in each article. When you return to a tab after checking something else, you jump straight back to where you were — not to the top of the page.

**Better for long-form reading.** Investigative articles, essays, and technical deep-dives benefit most. A 5,000-word article interrupted by several native ad placements is much easier to follow when each ad is clearly labelled.

## How ContentCue Identifies Ad Content

ContentCue uses a combination of techniques to classify page regions:

**Structural signals** — Ad containers typically use identifiable class names, iframe structures, or wrapper divs that don't appear in editorial content. ContentCue recognises these patterns.

**Position signals** — Ads in article content typically appear at predictable intervals (after every nth paragraph), in sidebars at standard widths, or in fixed positions at the top and bottom of articles.

**Network signals** — Requests to known ad-serving domains are cross-referenced against the page regions they populate.

When a region is classified as an ad, ContentCue draws a coloured border around it. You can configure the border colour in settings — the default is a soft amber that's visible without being distracting.

## Who Benefits

**News readers** who read multiple long articles per day will notice the most benefit. Native advertising in news sites tends to be the most convincing — professionally written, topically adjacent, and visually identical to editorial content.

**Research readers** who follow citation trails often end up on unfamiliar sites. ContentCue's ad labelling provides an immediate signal about which content on the page is organic vs. sponsored.

**Non-English speaking readers** who read in multiple languages simultaneously. With 11-language support, [ContentCue](/en/contentcue/) now adapts to your primary language regardless of which language the web content itself is in.

**Readers with limited time** benefit from knowing exactly where the article ends. Instead of reading several paragraphs before realising it's a sponsored piece, ContentCue's border appears immediately on page load.

## Privacy

ContentCue performs all ad classification locally, on your device. No browsing data, page content, or reading habits are uploaded to any server. The app doesn't require an account and doesn't include any analytics.
